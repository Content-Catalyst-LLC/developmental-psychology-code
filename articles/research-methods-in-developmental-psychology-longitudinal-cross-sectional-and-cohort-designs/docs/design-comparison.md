# Design Comparison

## Cross-sectional design

A cross-sectional design observes different people at different ages at one time. It is efficient, but age differences may partly reflect cohort differences.

## Longitudinal design

A longitudinal design observes the same people repeatedly. It can estimate within-person change, but attrition, practice effects, period shocks, and long timelines complicate interpretation.

## Cohort design

A cohort design compares people born or entering a developmental stage at different historical times. It can reveal historical differences but cannot automatically separate development from social change.

## Cohort-sequential design

A cohort-sequential or accelerated longitudinal design follows multiple cohorts over overlapping age ranges. It can estimate longer developmental trajectories in less calendar time, but requires careful modeling.

## Age-period-cohort problem

Age, period, and cohort are linearly related:

```text
cohort = period - age
```

Because of this relationship, researchers must interpret age, period, and cohort claims carefully.
