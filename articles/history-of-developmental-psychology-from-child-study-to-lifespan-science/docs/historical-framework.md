# Historical Framework

## Field-history concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Paradigm share | Synthetic relative prominence of a research tradition |
| Institutional support | Journals, societies, funding, training, and public agencies |
| Methodological advantage | Observational, experimental, longitudinal, psychometric, neurodevelopmental, or statistical capacity |
| Social relevance | Public concern, policy relevance, demographic pressure, educational demand, clinical need |
| Critique index | Ethical, conceptual, cultural, methodological, and political challenge |
| Lifespan index | Attention to adulthood and aging alongside childhood and adolescence |
| Broadening index | Lifespan, ecological, systems, cultural, disability, inequality, and power-aware expansion |

## Central historical claim

Developmental psychology's history is not only the rise and fall of theories. It is a history of changing scale: from child observation to lifespan development, from isolated child to ecology, from stages to systems, from universalized norms to more accountable attention to culture, inequality, disability, trauma, and history.
