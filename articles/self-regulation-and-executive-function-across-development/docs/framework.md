# Self-Regulation and Executive Function Framework

## Core developmental claims

1. Self-regulation is not mere obedience; it is the organization of attention, emotion, memory, inhibition, flexibility, and behavior toward goals.
2. Executive function includes cognitive control processes such as inhibition, working memory, and flexibility.
3. Regulation begins as co-regulation and becomes increasingly internalized across development.
4. Hot executive function differs from cool executive function because emotional and social pressure change regulatory demands.
5. Stress, sleep, caregiving, classroom design, disability support, and institutional climate shape regulation.
6. Regulatory struggle should be interpreted developmentally before being moralized.
7. Interventions and scaffolds matter because regulation is strengthened by repeated supported practice.

## Analytical structure

A general regulation trajectory can be represented as:

```text
E_it = rho * E_i,t-1 + beta * time + gamma * support_it - delta * stress_it + error_it
```

Where:

- `E_it` = regulation or executive-function outcome for person `i` at time `t`;
- `E_i,t-1` = prior regulatory functioning;
- `support_it` = caregiving, classroom, school, sleep, or intervention support;
- `stress_it` = acute stress, chronic stress, overload, threat, or fatigue.

Contextual fit can be represented as:

```text
E_it = alpha_i + beta * time + gamma * support_it + eta * temperament_i * support_it - delta * stress_it + error_it
```

This captures differential sensitivity: some children may benefit especially strongly from supportive environments and struggle more sharply in high-stress or poorly matched settings.
