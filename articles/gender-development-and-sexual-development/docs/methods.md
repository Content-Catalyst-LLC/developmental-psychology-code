# Methods

## Overview

This workflow models gender development and sexual development as distinct but interacting developmental processes. The synthetic data include adolescents observed repeatedly across waves and nested within school contexts.

The model includes:

- pubertal progression;
- family support;
- social recognition;
- consent knowledge;
- school connectedness;
- school climate;
- health education quality;
- anti-harassment support;
- chronic stigma;
- time-varying stigma exposure;
- protective context;
- broad adolescent adjustment.

## Conceptual interpretation

### Pubertal progression

Pubertal progression represents embodied developmental timing. It is not treated as a sufficient explanation for adolescent adjustment.

### Family support

Family support represents relational safety, communication, dignity, and developmental containment.

### Social recognition

Social recognition represents whether young people are understood, respected, and not punished for identity or expression.

### Consent knowledge

Consent knowledge represents age-appropriate understanding of boundaries, communication, bodily autonomy, and sexual health.

### School connectedness

School connectedness represents belonging, peer safety, supportive adults, and institutional inclusion.

### Stigma exposure

Stigma exposure represents harassment, exclusion, shame, misinformation, coercive pressure, or social sanction.

### Protective context

Protective context aggregates support, recognition, consent knowledge, connectedness, school climate, health education quality, and anti-harassment support.

## Synthetic-data caution

The data are not empirical findings. They are simulated for reproducible demonstration only.
